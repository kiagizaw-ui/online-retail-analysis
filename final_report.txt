Online Retail Data Analysis — Final Report
📌 Overview

This analysis explores customer purchasing behavior, revenue distribution, and retention patterns using transactional retail data. Multiple advanced analytical techniques were applied, including aggregation, percentile analysis, outlier investigation, and cohort analysis, to uncover the underlying drivers of revenue.

📊 Insight 1: Geographic Revenue Concentration Driven by Customer Distribution

The United Kingdom generates approximately 3.26 million in revenue, vastly exceeding all other countries, with the next highest contributors (EIRE and Germany) producing less than 100,000 each. This indicates an extreme geographic imbalance in total revenue contribution.

However, this dominance is not necessarily due to higher individual customer spending, but rather a significantly larger volume of transactions and customers within the UK. Smaller markets, such as the Netherlands and Japan, generate relatively strong revenue despite fewer transactions, suggesting higher per-customer value in certain regions.

📌 Implication

Revenue concentration is primarily driven by customer distribution rather than inherent regional performance differences, indicating that expansion strategies should focus on increasing customer base in underrepresented regions.

📊 Insight 2: Product Revenue Concentration Driven by High-Value Customer Behavior

Revenue is highly concentrated among a small number of products, with the top product generating approximately 168,469, more than double the second-ranked product at around 62,383. This sharp drop-off indicates that product performance is not evenly distributed.

This pattern suggests that top-performing products are not broadly popular across all customers but are instead driven by repeated purchases from a small subset of high-value customers. The same products frequently appear in high-value transactions, reinforcing this concentration effect.

📌 Implication

Product success is strongly influenced by customer purchasing behavior rather than widespread demand, meaning targeting high-value customers is more impactful than expanding product variety.

📊 Insight 3: Extreme Revenue Concentration Among Top Customers

Analysis shows that the top 10% of customers contribute approximately 59.1% of total revenue, indicating a highly skewed revenue distribution. Individual top customers generate over 100,000 in revenue, while the majority contribute relatively small amounts.

This imbalance explains both country-level and product-level concentration patterns, as high-value customers disproportionately influence total revenue and purchasing trends.

📌 Implication

Business performance is heavily dependent on a small group of high-value customers, making customer retention and targeted engagement critical for sustained revenue growth.

📊 Insight 4: Outliers Reflect Bulk Purchasing Behavior Rather Than Data Errors

Outlier analysis identified 12,968 high-value transactions, with some exceeding 168,000 in revenue and involving extremely large quantities (e.g., 80,995 units). These transactions are consistently associated with specific customers and products.

This indicates that outliers are not random anomalies but represent a distinct group of bulk or wholesale buyers operating at a different scale than typical customers.

📌 Implication

Revenue patterns are shaped by two distinct customer segments (regular vs bulk buyers), requiring differentiated strategies for each group.

📊 Insight 5: Low Initial Retention with Strong Long-Term Value from a Small Segment

Cohort analysis shows a sharp decline in customer retention after the first month. For example, in one cohort, 330 initial customers drop to around 45–62 in subsequent months, indicating a retention rate of approximately 13–18%.

Despite this early drop-off, a small group of customers continues to make purchases over extended periods, contributing significantly to long-term revenue. This aligns with the presence of high-value and repeat customers identified earlier.

📌 Implication

Customer behavior is divided between short-term buyers and long-term high-value customers, suggesting that improving early retention is key to increasing overall revenue.

 Final Conclusion

The analysis reveals that revenue is not evenly distributed across customers, products, or regions. Instead, it is driven by a small group of high-value customers whose purchasing behavior creates concentrated patterns across all dimensions. Understanding and targeting this segment is essential for optimizing business performance.
